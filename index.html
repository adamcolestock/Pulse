Monika Lin

The shifting shapes are derivations/extractions of longitude and latitude coordinates from a stretch of the New Jersey Shoreline at Cape May. The numerical information is separated into a series of xy coordinates in P5 as corresponding abstract shapes, rather than visualized as a linear segment of a shoreline.

Fluid Boundaries

by Adam Colestock

One contour classified as "URBAN" and one contour classified as "WETLANDS" forming the coastline of New Jersey are selected at random and placed on the canvas above their category label. The contours slowly flow and blend together forming a continuous non-binary abstract form suggesting that simple category labels might limit human thinking about the relationship between the natural and the built environment along our coasts.

See the about page for more information about the dataset that was used.

This piece was inspired by this video that led me to think about how, in a time in which climate change is leading to dangerously shifting coastlines, we might need to question our assumptions about the strategies and benefits of 'softening' vs. 'hardening' our coastlines to 'defend' against natural forces
